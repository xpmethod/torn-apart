    The #RedForEd movement drew more than 50,000 people to the Capitol, closed more than 1,000 schools and displaced more than 850,000 students last week.

    As of Sunday afternoon, teachers and other school employeesinvolved in the walkoutshowed no signs of slowing down.

    Here's what to expect when the walkout resumes Monday.

      Most schools will closeMonday

      Many of the more than 1,000 schools shuttered last week will remain closed Monday.

      The list includes the state's 10 largest school districts:Mesa, Tucson, Chandler, Peoria, Gilbert, Deer Valley, Paradise Valley, Dysart, Phoenix Union and Washington Elementary.

      MORE: Here are the Arizona schools closing when teachers walk out Monday

      Protestsat Capitol will continue

      Joe Thomas, Arizona Education Association president, announced toa crowd of thousands at the Arizona Capitol on Friday that teachers would returnMonday.

      Protests will also reach beyond metro Phoenix.

      Hundreds of people rallied in downtown Flagstaff and Tucson last week and plan to return Monday. Randi Weingarten, president of the American Federation of Teachers, plans to travel to Arizona tohost a news conference Monday morning.

      Angela Murphy (center) joins fellow Arizona teachers during a rally at the Arizona state Capitol for higher pay and more education funding on April 27, 2018, in Phoenix. (Photo: Rob Schumacher/The Republic)

      Some schools will reopen

      Classes at some smaller schooldistricts, including Wickenburg Unified School District and Tolleson Union High School District,will resume Monday.

      Chandler Unified School District initially announced it would reopen Monday but later changed course, saying that "based on the number of teachers who have reported their absence for Monday, our schools will need toremain closed April 30th."

      Scottsdale Unified School District also announced it would reopen Tuesday, but said Sunday afternoon that it would not "due to the high number of teachers reporting absences and the shortage of substitutes to cover those absences."

      Schools that attempt to reopen before education leaders call for an end to the statewide walkoutmay face widespread "sick-ins," with staff takingsick days in protest.

      CLOSE

      Gov. Doug Ducey talked with reporters about the #RedForEd walkout April 26, 2018. Carly Henry/azcentral.com The Republic | azcentral.com

      Lawmakers may approve a deal

      Gov. Doug Ducey on Friday announced he had secured a deal with state lawmakers to approve his proposed pay increase for teachers, but he did not provide any details.

      Officials said they would be "working through the weekend" and would introduce the plan in the Legislature "early next week and pass it shortlythereafter."

      Thomas, the Arizona Education Association president, and Noah Karvelis, a teacher and Arizona Educators United organizer, issued a joint statement saying they didn't trust the governor's announcement.

      "We have a press release and a tweet from the governor," they said. "We have no bill. We have no deal. The devil is in the details."

      CLOSE

      Joshua Buckley, a teacher from Red Mountain High School, discusses an initiative filed with the Secretary of State's office on April 27, 2018, that proposes to fund education through an increase in income tax rates. Wochit

      Education leadersweigh solutions

      On Friday, education leaders launched a ballot initiative called theInvest in Education Act.

      The measure would give voters the opportunity to decide in November whether they want to fund public education with higher income taxes on people who earn more than $250,000.

      It was not immediately clear which of the groups protesting low teacher pay may support the initiative. Organizations will likely commenton the measure this week.
